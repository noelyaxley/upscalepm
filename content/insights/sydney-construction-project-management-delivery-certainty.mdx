## 1. Define feasibility around *deliverability*, not just yield

On Sydney projects, feasibility fails most often when the model assumes “best-case” delivery—then gets exposed by latent site constraints, services upgrades, access limitations, contamination, or conditions of consent. A yield-led approach can look strong on paper, but a deliverability-led approach is what survives tendering and construction.

The industry backdrop matters: ABS construction price indices show that building input and output prices lifted sharply through 2021–2023, and even with moderation, trade packages typically don’t return to pre-2020 pricing levels. That means feasibility needs real allowances, packaging logic, and procurement timing baked in from day one—particularly for civils, services, façade and lifts.

> “If you can’t articulate *how* it will be built, you don’t have a feasibility—just a concept.”  
> - UpScale PM, Project Advisory Team

**Takeaway:** Build feasibility from a delivery plan: scope clarity, risk allowances, services strategy and procurement sequencing—before you optimise yield.

## 2. Treat approvals as a program risk—then actively manage it

In Sydney, approvals aren’t just a hurdle; they are a *schedule driver*. Delays compound quickly because holding costs, escalation and resequencing can erode contingency faster than most spreadsheets reflect. A robust DA strategy is not simply good planning practice—it is commercial risk control.

This is where real data becomes crucial. For example, NSW planning pathways can include local DA, State Significant Development (SSD), VPA negotiations, design excellence processes, and referral agency requirements—all of which influence program certainty. Without the original article, we can’t cite which pathway “Selected Topic 4” sits under, but the lesson remains: approvals need an integrated plan that links consultant deliverables, authority interactions, and document control to the overall development program.

> “Approvals aren’t a separate workstream. They’re the front end of construction.”  
> - UpScale PM, Development Management

**Takeaway:** Put DA strategy on the master program with clear gates, owners and decision timeframes—then manage it like a critical path activity.

## 3. Procurement strategy must match finance and delivery risk

In the current market, the difference between a viable and non-viable project often comes down to procurement structure: early contractor involvement, trade packaging, escalation clauses, and how risk is shared. Funding partners are also more sensitive to construction risk allocation—particularly where program and cost certainty are not well evidenced.

Construction cost movement since 2020 (as indicated through ABS price measures) reinforces the need for procurement that reduces exposure to late-stage “sticker shock”. Where design is immature, going hard-lump-sum too early can lead to claims and variations; where design is mature and interfaces are controlled, it can lock in certainty.

> “The goal isn’t the cheapest contract form—it’s the most financeable and deliverable structure for the site.”  
> - UpScale PM, Project Delivery Lead

**Takeaway:** Align procurement to design maturity, lender requirements and interface risk—then document assumptions and exclusions so the tender result is comparable and fundable.

## 4. Control interfaces early: services, civils, and neighbours

On dense Sydney sites, interfaces are where projects bleed time and money: service authority upgrades, stormwater constraints, crane oversail, traffic management, hoardings, temporary works, and neighbour access. If these aren’t locked down early, you get design churn, latent conditions, and approvals friction during construction—right when you need certainty.

The broader data story supports this: higher construction prices amplify the cost of rework, delay and acceleration. Even “small” interface mistakes—like late utility authority lead times or under-scoped temporary works—become expensive when trade rates and preliminaries are elevated.

> “Interfaces aren’t ‘site problems’—they’re preconstruction problems that show up late.”  
> - UpScale PM, Construction Advisory

**Takeaway:** Run an interface register in preconstruction (authorities, neighbours, access, temporary works) and close items before you commit to program dates.

## 5. Governance is what keeps a project bankable and buildable

Developers don’t lose projects because they lack intent—they lose them because decisions are late, changes are unmanaged, and reporting doesn’t show the truth early enough. The fix is disciplined governance: fortnightly cost-to-complete, program risk reviews, change control, and consultant performance management with clear deliverables.

In a market where pricing has moved significantly since 2020 (per ABS construction price measures), governance is not “admin”—it’s how you protect contingency and keep stakeholders aligned. This becomes especially important when lenders require increased transparency on drawdowns, variations, and progress, and when purchasers (or end users) expect premium outcomes even as costs rise.

> “Good governance doesn’t slow delivery—it prevents the rework that slows delivery.”  
> - UpScale PM, Project Controls

**Takeaway:** Build a governance rhythm that forces early truth: cost-to-complete, change control, risk burn-down, and decision logs tied to authority.

## Conclusion

The central question behind “Selected Topic 4” is the same one every Sydney developer, investor and landowner is asking right now: *how do we deliver a project with confidence when costs, approvals and interfaces are harder to control?* The answer is not a single silver bullet—it’s a delivery system: feasibility grounded in buildability, approvals treated as a critical path, procurement matched to finance, interface risks closed early, and governance that keeps decisions timely and transparent.
