# Execute Your Burwood North rezoning DA strategy: Lessons from Burwood North Metro Precinct Rezoning, Sydney

![NSW Government official logo](/images/insights/burwood-north-rezoning/hero.jpg)  
*Image source: NSW Government / Minister Paul Scully Media Release*

Sydney’s next wave of housing supply is increasingly being shaped by state-led rezoning around rapid transit—because the market has little slack left. In the Inner West, vacancy rates are sitting at **below 1.5%**, while median apartment prices are around **$950,000 (Q4 2025)**—a sharp signal that demand is outpacing deliverable stock in well-located precincts. Against that backdrop, the NSW Government is accelerating **transit oriented development** through a pipeline of **68 statewide rezonings** intended to unlock **236,000 homes** and **167,000 jobs**.

The **Burwood North Metro Precinct Rezoning** is a flagship example. The draft, state-led proposal covers **113 hectares** within **800 metres** of the future **Burwood North Metro Station** (Sydney Metro West), and targets up to **18,300 new homes** and **3,900 jobs**—a **3,000+ home increase** on the earlier August 2025 plan of 15,000 homes. Buildings are flagged at **8–42 storeys**, with **5–10% affordable housing in perpetuity**, alongside upgrades to open space, roads, and walking/cycling connections.

For developers, investors, and landowners, the question is simple: how do you build a DA strategy now that wins later?

---

## 1. Burwood North rezoning lifts yield—if you read the envelope early

The headline number—**18,300 homes**—is what grabs attention, but your DA strategy should start with the *controls logic* behind that uplift. The draft rezoning is designed to maximise density within **800 metres** of a Metro station that’s due to open in **2032**, with building heights proposed up to **42 storeys**. That’s effectively a pre-signal of where NSW planning wants height, massing, and intensity concentrated, and where it expects a more tapered response.

State-led precincts can reduce some traditional DA risks by making heights and density more predictable, but they also introduce new “precinct compliance” issues: affordable housing quotas (**5–10% in perpetuity**), new public domain expectations, and infrastructure interfaces that can materially affect buildable yield and timing.

> "The Burwood North rezoning proposal allows for more than 18,000 new homes and nearly 4000 jobs in a thriving neighbourhood close to world class public transport."  
> - Paul Scully, Minister for Planning

**Takeaway:** Treat the draft controls as a *future DA brief*. Start test-fitting compliant schemes now (height, setbacks, wind, overshadowing, public domain) so you can move quickly once the rezoning is finalised.

## 2. Your DA strategy must be exhibition-proof, not just code-compliant

The precinct is on public exhibition from **10 March to 6–10 April 2026** (as advised across government and council channels), progressing via a **State Significant Rezoning** pathway. That exhibition window is not just a formality—it’s where yield assumptions can be challenged, and where the final “shape” of the planning framework often gets negotiated.

UpScale PM has seen exhibition feedback materially reshape outcomes in other TOD-style rezonings—particularly on interfaces (existing low-rise neighbours, heritage edges, school catchments) and on local infrastructure capacity (traffic, kerbside management, open space). The non-obvious risk is that even where the precinct’s headline capacity remains intact, a subset of sites can be moderated, re-stacked, or pushed into later staging—changing feasibility dramatically.

> "Rezoning Burwood North unlocks a once-in-a-generation opportunity to deliver a more connected, vibrant and people-focused centre."  
> - John Faker, Mayor of Burwood

Use the exhibition period to your advantage: formal submissions that are solutions-focused (not self-interested) tend to land better—e.g., endorsing density while proposing clear mitigation measures (loading, access, park delivery sequencing, active transport links).

**Takeaway:** Build an “exhibition-proof” DA pathway: prepare alternate massing scenarios, staging logic, and mitigation commitments so you’re resilient if 10–20% of your assumed yield shifts during finalisation.

---

## 3. Transit oriented development wins—when the program matches Metro reality

The Sydney Metro precinct narrative is compelling: Burwood North is being planned around a future station enabling **10-minute trips to Sydney CBD and Parramatta**, with the station opening targeted for **2032**. For investors, that accessibility drives end value; for developers, it changes what the market will accept in apartment mix, podium activation, and workplace/retail integration.

But TOD success is not automatic. The sequencing between the station, enabling works, utilities, and early private development determines whether the first buildings feel like a “connected centre” or isolated towers waiting for transport and public domain to catch up. Precincts of this scale—**113ha across Burwood and City of Canada Bay LGAs**—also demand stakeholder alignment: state agencies, councils, utility authorities, and multiple landowners.

> "This rezoning proposal will unlock thousands of new homes and jobs in our local community all within walking distance of the Burwood North Metro Station."  
> - Jason Yat-Sen Li, Member for Strathfield

**Takeaway:** Align your DA strategy to the Metro program. Bake in staging that can stand alone *before* 2032, but can also “plug in” seamlessly to the station-opening uplift (public domain, wayfinding, ground plane activation, and transport interfaces).

---

## 4. Feasibility in Burwood North rezoning is a cost-and-capital equation

Precinct rezonings can create significant value uplift through additional GFA and height, but feasibility still lives or dies on delivery costs, funding terms, and product velocity. In Sydney’s Inner West, high-rise residential delivery is benchmarking at roughly **$3,500–$4,500 per sqm** (industry benchmarks cited in the brief), and the market remains sensitive to capital settings with the **RBA cash rate at 4.35% (Mar 2026)**.

That matters because higher rates typically tighten pre-sales requirements and reduce buyer borrowing capacity, especially for higher-density product. If your feasibility assumes “market will absorb it because it’s near Metro”, you can end up with a DA-approved project that is unfinanceable when you want to start.

Affordable housing is also not a footnote here: the draft settings include **5–10% affordable housing in perpetuity**. Whether delivered on-site, via a managed provider, or through an alternative mechanism (subject to policy settings), it impacts revenue, design efficiency, and operating complexity.

> "The Burwood North rezoning proposal allows for more than 18,000 new homes and nearly 4000 jobs in a thriving neighbourhood close to world class public transport."  
> - Paul Scully, Minister for Planning

**Takeaway:** Run feasibility with three levers from day one—cost inflation sensitivity, pre-sale/settlement timing, and affordable housing delivery structure—so your DA strategy delivers a *buildable* approval, not just an impressive one.

## 5. The Burwood North rezoning DA “gold rush” will reward early movers

Because this is a **state-led** rezoning, no individual developer, architect, or builder is being announced yet—this is a framework intended to enable *future* DAs. That’s exactly why early positioning matters. Once the rezoning is final, the market typically compresses: land prices re-rate quickly, design teams get booked out, and councils/agencies become inundated with pre-lodgement requests.

The proposal’s scale—**18,300 homes and 3,900 jobs**—signals that NSW planning expects Burwood North to function as a genuine centre, not a collection of residential towers. Developers who incorporate jobs, services, and high-quality ground plane outcomes tend to have an easier run through design review, agency coordination, and community scrutiny (particularly in high-visibility precincts along the Parramatta Road corridor).

It also pays to think beyond single-site optimisation. With a precinct spanning two LGAs (Burwood and Canada Bay), multi-site options (or partnerships) can create staging flexibility and reduce risk if infrastructure delivery or policy settings shift between exhibition and finalisation.

> "Rezoning Burwood North unlocks a once-in-a-generation opportunity to deliver a more connected, vibrant and people-focused centre."  
> - John Faker, Mayor of Burwood

**Takeaway:** Position now for post-rezoning speed: secure site control where possible, develop precinct-compliant concept options, and plan staging/approvals so you can lodge quickly once the controls are settled.

## Conclusion: DA strategy is the difference between upside and overreach

The **Burwood North rezoning** is a clear signal that **NSW planning** is doubling down on **transit oriented development** across the **Sydney Metro precinct** network—using state-led controls to unlock major housing capacity where transport can carry it. But capacity on paper doesn’t automatically translate into development success. The winners will be those who treat the rezoning as a commercial and delivery framework: designing for compliance, exhibition risk, infrastructure reality, and financing constraints—before the broader market rushes in.

If you’re a landowner or developer in (or near) the **Burwood North Metro Precinct Rezoning** area, now is the time to move from “watching the rezoning” to building a bankable DA plan.

- **Model multiple compliant envelopes now** (including a moderated option) to protect feasibility through exhibition changes.  
- **Align staging to the 2032 station opening**, so early stages work independently but benefit from the Metro uplift later.  
- **Treat affordable housing and infrastructure as core feasibility inputs**, not post-DA surprises.
