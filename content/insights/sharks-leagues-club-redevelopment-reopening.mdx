# Reopen Stronger: Lessons from Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment

![Julien Bova, newly appointed GM of Sharks Leagues Club](/images/insights/sharks-leagues-club/hero.jpg)  
*Image: Club Management (clubmanagement.com.au)*

NSW clubs are redeveloping with real urgency. With NSW registered clubs generating an estimated **$6.5B in revenue (2024)** and hospitality up **~5% year-on-year**, boards are doubling down on venues that can compete for dining, functions, and entertainment spend—while navigating tighter operating conditions and looming reforms. At the same time, overall membership remains broadly **flat at around 1.2 million**, so the “build it and they will come” assumption no longer holds. Redevelopment needs to be staged, bankable, and operationally grounded.

That context is exactly why the **Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment** at Woolooware Bay matters. After closing in **December 2019**, the club is now on a path to reopen **mid-to-late 2026**, following a complex journey that included COVID disruption, cost escalations, and prolonged negotiations before the project could properly restart. The new three-level venue is being delivered within an existing shell handed over by developers **Novm and Capital Corporation (July 2025)**, with **Xenia Constructions** leading the build and fit-out and **CARAS** providing project management.

For club boards planning a reopening in NSW, this project offers practical lessons worth copying—and a few pitfalls worth avoiding.

## 1. Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment: plan for the long road (and member fatigue)

Sharks Leagues Club has lived the scenario many boards fear: a high-profile closure, then delays that stretch beyond a normal capital works cycle. The club shut its doors in **December 2019**, and only regained practical momentum after developers **Novm and Capital Corporation** handed over the base building shell in **July 2025**—years after the original disruption. Delivery then moved into a defined programme: **site establishment mid‑August 2025**, **façade demolition mid‑September 2025**, and steady progress through early 2026.

The lesson isn’t simply “delays happen”. It’s that a reopening strategy must treat communications and confidence as a workstream. In an environment where NSW club membership is broadly stable at **~1.2M**, member loyalty can’t be assumed during long closures. When timelines move, clubs need an agreed cadence of updates and clear milestones that members can understand (for example: handover, demolition, services rough-in, fit-out commencement, commissioning, soft launch).

> "This has been a long and complex journey, but today marks a defining moment... from COVID disruptions to cost escalations."  
> - Steve Mace, Chairman, Sharks

**Takeaway:** If your club reopening NSW timeline may stretch, mandate a board-approved communications plan with milestone-based updates—so member confidence doesn’t become a latent project risk.

## 2. Design to win the market, not just replace the old club

The most bankable club redevelopments don’t rebuild yesterday’s venue; they reset the product to match current spend patterns—especially in hospitality, where NSW clubs have seen **~5% year-on-year growth**. Sharks’ brief is clearly shaped around growth drivers: a **three-level facility** with the main operating floor (Level 3) incorporating bistro, café, gaming, and a chairman’s lounge—plus a major signature feature, an **1,800 sqm north-facing alfresco deck** with views across Woolooware Bay and the stadium.

Upstairs, the functions strategy is unambiguous: a top floor with conference/function spaces, including a **largest room capacity of 550 people**. That scale signals a strong focus on conferences, celebrations, and game-day corporate demand—revenue streams that can outperform “general trade” when executed well.

For boards, the practical point is this: the CapEx-to-revenue link needs to be explicit early. A deck of **1,800 sqm** and a function room for **550** are not “nice-to-haves”; they drive staffing models, acoustic/fire strategies, vertical transport requirements, and ultimately the operating budget.

> "The Club build will take around 12-15 months to complete, with a projected opening date of mid-to-late 2026."  
> - Dino Mezzatesta, CEO, Sharks Group

**Takeaway:** Tie every major design move to a commercial outcome (functions, premium dining, alfresco trade, game-day spend) and test it against your staffing and operating model before you lock scope.

## 3. Shell handovers can save months—if you interrogate what you’re inheriting

A defining feature of the **Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment** is the delivery model: the club is being rebuilt within an **existing shell structure** handed over by the developers in **July 2025**. On paper, that can be a huge programme advantage, often cutting time and preliminaries compared with a full ground-up build. The project then moved quickly into enabling works: site establishment and façade demolition, then progress on **glazing, cladding, structural steel, concrete infills, and internal fire protection** by **February 2026**.

But shell handovers are also where hidden risks live—especially around waterproofing interfaces, service risers, fire compartments, plant space, and access for fit-out trades. For clubs, those risks manifest late as variations, commissioning delays, or compromised back-of-house layouts. This is exactly where disciplined client-side project management adds value: front-loading due diligence, clarifying what is “base build” versus “fit-out”, and documenting interfaces so the build doesn’t become a debate.

The industry context matters: hospitality/club fit-outs commonly benchmark at **$3,500–$5,000/sqm** (NSW, 2025 guide ranges). If you inherit a shell that isn’t truly fit-out-ready, that rate can spike quickly.

> "Works... have continued to progress steadily... glazing and cladding works to the northern façade are now underway."  
> - CARAS Project Management

**Takeaway:** Treat shell handover as a formal gate: complete forensic due diligence on waterproofing, risers, fire strategy, and access—before fit-out packages are released.

## 4. Staged commissioning (and the right contract) is how you reopen with confidence

Even where a club remains closed throughout construction, staged commissioning is what protects the reopening date. Sharks’ programme shows a logical sequencing: demolition and external works first, then building envelope and services progression, then fit-out activities ramping through 2026. With a target to reopen **mid-to-late 2026** (aligned to the **second half of the NRL season**), commissioning can’t be left as a last-minute scramble; it needs to be planned as a series of “ready-for-test” milestones (fire systems, essential services, kitchen extraction, gaming compliance, lift commissioning, acoustic sign-off, and BCA compliance).

From a governance standpoint, this is where **AS4902-style** thinking (even where a club uses alternative forms) helps boards: define scope cleanly, allocate risk deliberately, and—critically—use **separable portions** or staged handover logic so parts of the venue can be tested, trained, and defected systematically. At UpScale PM, we’re applying similar staged-delivery discipline on heritage-sensitive club projects (such as the Granville Diggers Club redevelopment) because it gives boards clarity and reduces “big bang opening” risk.

> "Site establishment is expected to commence around mid-August... demolition will commence to the façade..."  
> - Gerard McNamara, Xenia Constructions

**Takeaway:** Build a staged commissioning plan into your master programme and contract approach—so compliance, training, and operations readiness are delivered in steps, not at the final whistle.

## 5. Protect revenue and culture while you’re closed (or disrupted)

Not every club can stay open during works. Sharks Leagues Club remained closed, but critically it maintained a presence through **temporary trading at Sharks at Kareela**. That is a strategic move—not just an operational convenience. It protects staff capability, keeps member habits alive, and maintains cashflow while the primary venue is off-line.

Boards often underestimate the organisational strain of a multi-year shutdown: key people leave, systems drift, and member expectations reset. Sharks addressed a major readiness risk by appointing a new GM, **Julien Bova**, in **February 2026**—ahead of the venue launch. Leadership timing matters: reopening isn’t just a construction milestone, it’s an operational transformation that needs recruitment, training, menu and function sales planning, and stakeholder engagement well before doors open.

This aligns with broader NSW realities: with overall membership flat (~**1.2M**) and competition from new precinct-style developments, clubs can’t afford to “go dark” and hope to regain momentum at opening.

> "The Club build will take around 12-15 months to complete, with a projected opening date of mid-to-late 2026."  
> - Dino Mezzatesta, CEO, Sharks Group

**Takeaway:** If your venue must close, plan a satellite trading and member engagement strategy from feasibility—then staff your reopening early so culture and service standards are ready on day one.

## Conclusion: what Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment teaches about reopening well in NSW

The central lesson from the **Sharks Leagues Club redevelopment** is that reopening successfully isn’t about construction progress alone—it’s about aligning scope, programme, and operations so the venue can trade confidently from day one. In a NSW market where clubs are investing into modern hospitality to defend revenue (amid reform pressure and flat membership), projects must be planned as commercial turnarounds, not just building works.

Sharks’ approach highlights the value of clear milestones (from shell handover in **July 2025** through to a targeted **mid-to-late 2026** opening), a market-facing venue mix (including the **1,800 sqm** alfresco deck and **550-person** function capacity), and operational continuity through a temporary trading solution.

For club boards planning a club reopening NSW, keep these priorities front and centre:

- **Design for revenue:** lock the hospitality and functions strategy early, and ensure CapEx decisions have a measurable operating rationale.  
- **De-risk the programme:** use staged commissioning logic (and where appropriate, **AS4902** tools like separable portions) to avoid a high-risk “big bang” opening.  
- **Maintain continuity:** protect trading, staff capability, and member engagement through satellite operations and disciplined communications.
