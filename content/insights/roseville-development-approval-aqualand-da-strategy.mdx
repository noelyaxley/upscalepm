# Win Roseville development approval: Lessons from Larkin Street, NSW

![Architectural render of Aqualand's two mid-rise apartment buildings (9-10 storeys) on sloping Roseville site.](/images/insights/roseville-development-approval/hero.jpg)
*Image credit: The Urban Developer / Woods Bagot*

Sydney’s planning battleground is shifting from “whether” to “how fast”—especially around rail. Under NSW Transport Oriented Development (TOD) settings, precincts within 400 metres of stations are being repositioned to carry more of the housing load, while councils try to retain local scale controls. In Ku-ring-gai alone, the revised TOD pipeline targets **24,728 homes by end‑2025**, a clear signal that state-led density outcomes are now baked into the system.

Aqualand Prestige’s **Larkin Street Roseville** proposal (SSD **77829461**) is a clean case study of what that shift looks like in practice. The state approved a **$93.4 million** development at **2–4 Larkin Street and 1–5 Pockley Avenue, Roseville**, overriding Ku-ring-gai Council’s objections on height and scale. The project replaces five detached houses on a **3,552 sqm** sloping site with **two 9–10 storey buildings** delivering **111 apartments**, including **30 affordable units** managed by Bridge Housing.

Below are the practical implications for your next DA strategy—especially if council refusal risk is on your critical path.

## 1. State override changed the risk profile for council refusal

The defining feature of Roseville is not just the built form—it’s the pathway. By securing approval as a **State Significant Development (SSD 77829461)**, Aqualand moved the decision away from local politics and into a state framework designed to prioritise housing supply. Ku-ring-gai Council objected to height and scale, yet the proposal was still approved under ministerial delegation by the Department’s Deputy Secretary. That’s not a loophole—it’s the NSW planning system operating as intended when state priorities (housing delivery and TOD) outweigh local refusal positions.

What developers should notice is timing. The approval leaned on the application’s alignment with TOD and state housing priorities, and it benefited from being lodged in the context of shifting controls. In other words: if your site sits in an emerging station precinct, your **DA strategy** can’t wait until “everything settles”. The Roseville decision reinforces that the state will back outcomes that are consistent with supply targets—provided your documentation is robust.

> “The Department found the proposal **‘consistent with state housing priorities and in the public interest.’**”  
> - NSW Department of Planning (assessment finding)

**Takeaway:** If council refusal is likely, assess SSD eligibility early and build a decision pathway that matches the state’s housing and TOD objectives—not just local controls.

## 2. TOD proximity is the real planning leverage in Roseville development approval

Roseville’s planning logic starts with one fact: the site sits **within 400 metres of Roseville Station**. That proximity is the trigger condition for TOD intensification and, increasingly, the justification used to defend height, yield and reduced car dependency. In precincts like the Upper North Shore, station adjacency is now a planning asset you can quantify and argue—particularly when the broader pipeline is explicit (again: **24,728 homes** flagged in Ku-ring-gai’s TOD plan by end‑2025).

For developers, this changes how you should frame your planning narrative. The core argument isn’t “this building is reasonable”; it’s “this site is strategically located to absorb density with managed impacts”. In Roseville, traffic and access impacts were considered acceptable with conditions—including a **Green Travel Plan**—which is a clear indicator that state agencies expect mode-shift commitments, not just more parking.

When we advise clients on **Roseville development approval** (and similar station precinct sites), we treat transport planning as a first-order approval driver. If your traffic, pedestrian and cycling strategy is an appendix rather than a headline, you’re leaving leverage on the table—and inviting late-stage conditions that can bite during delivery.

> “This project embodies a broader social responsibility—helping shape a more diverse, resilient and connected community for the future.”  
> - Project design report, Aqualand / Planning & Co.

**Takeaway:** In TOD catchments, lead with station proximity, mode-shift commitments and precinct outcomes—then defend built form as the logical consequence.

## 3. The numbers matter: feasibility, delivery, and the affordable housing lever

Roseville is a reminder that approvals follow numbers as much as drawings. The project proposes **111 apartments** across two mid-rise buildings, with **30 affordable units**—a substantial **27%** affordable housing component—managed by Bridge Housing. That ratio does two things: it strengthens the state-interest case and it helps de-risk objections that would otherwise focus on “overdevelopment”.

On feasibility, the benchmark is sobering. Using the HIA Q4 2025 Sydney guide, mid-rise residential construction sits around **$6,000–$6,500/sqm**; the brief pegs an estimated **$6,200/sqm** for this typology. Against a $93.4 million project, any post-approval condition that adds complexity (retaining trees, staging constraints, services upgrades, premium façade adjustments) can meaningfully move the needle.

Aqualand also reduced funding pressure via joint-venture equity with Phoenix Property Investors—material in a higher-rate market where the **RBA cash rate was 4.35% (Mar 2026)** and pre-sales are harder to underwrite. This is exactly where project management intersects planning: the “right” approval is the one that stays feasible after conditions, not just on lodgement day.

> “Securing our first end-to-end equity investment under the Aqualand Prestige brand is a major milestone... allowing us to deliver smaller scale residential projects with the same level of design excellence.”  
> - Jin Lin, Managing Director, Aqualand Group

**Takeaway:** Model planning conditions into feasibility from day one—especially where affordable housing commitments, interest rates and construction benchmarks tighten your margin for error.

## 4. DA strategy in the NSW planning system: timing + evidence wins

Roseville’s approval points to a playbook developers should internalise: **timing, alignment, and evidence**. The application’s timing—relative to council’s evolving TOD controls—mattered. But timing alone doesn’t get an SSD over the line. The approval relied on comprehensive technical work typical for SSD assessment: traffic modelling (Varga), wind (Windtech), overshadowing, stormwater and design compliance against the Apartment Design Guide (with only minor variances noted in the brief).

This is where many projects fail: they aim to “win the argument” with council rather than “prove the outcome” to the state. SSD assessment is less about debating preferences and more about demonstrating that impacts are understood, mitigated and conditioned. Roseville’s conditions (including the Green Travel Plan and controls around stormwater and tree removal) are also a warning: if you haven’t pre-modelled delivery constraints, your cost plan can blow out after approval.

Our non-obvious lesson from the brief is especially relevant for the Upper North Shore: conditions that lock down tree removal or construction methodology can add **5–10%** to project management and delivery costs if you’re forced into bespoke retention, protection zones, or redesign during detailed documentation.

**Takeaway:** In the NSW planning system, treat SSD like a proof exercise—front-load technical evidence and cost the conditions before they cost you.

## 5. What Roseville signals: state significant development is now a delivery tool

The bigger message in Roseville is directional: state intervention is becoming a practical delivery tool for projects that meet strategic housing objectives—even in politically sensitive suburbs. With a documented housing shortfall (Urbis flagged **20,000+ dwellings** in 2025, per the brief) and a low-vacancy market (around **1.5% on the North Shore**, per the brief), the NSW Government has strong incentive to keep supply moving.

For landowners and developers in Ku-ring-gai and the Upper North Shore, this changes negotiation dynamics. A council refusal is no longer the end of the story if you have: (1) TOD proximity, (2) a credible affordability component, and (3) an SSD-ready technical package. Roseville also highlights delivery sequencing risk: the project is expected to be built **in one stage**, which can reduce holding costs—but it amplifies cashflow pressure if pre-sales timing slips (sales are anticipated **later in 2026**, per the brief).

This is why we view **state significant development** not just as planning strategy, but as project strategy. It affects programme, funding structure, procurement approach, and even how you message the project to community and stakeholders.

> “This project embodies a broader social responsibility—helping shape a more diverse, resilient and connected community for the future.”  
> - Project design report, Aqualand / Planning & Co.

**Takeaway:** Use SSD to manage refusal risk—but only if your commercial plan (funding, staging, pre-sales) is designed to survive the conditions and the timetable.

## Conclusion

Aqualand’s Roseville outcome answers the central question behind “council overridden”: yes, the state can and will approve projects over local objection—but only where the proposal clearly advances TOD and housing priorities and is backed by rigorous evidence. For developers chasing **Roseville development approval** or similar station-precinct outcomes, the project signals that the NSW planning system is increasingly outcome-driven: deliver density in the right place, with manageable impacts, and the state will provide a pathway.

What to take into your next DA strategy:

- **Pick the right pathway early:** if refusal risk is high, test SSD viability before you lock design, consultants and programme.  
- **Build a TOD-first narrative:** station proximity and mode shift (e.g., Green Travel Plans) are now core approval logic.  
- **Cost the conditions upfront:** approvals that add delivery constraints can erode feasibility fast—especially at **~$6,200/sqm** mid-rise benchmarks.

If your site sits near rail in the Upper North Shore, the window is open—but it rewards speed, planning discipline, and delivery realism.
